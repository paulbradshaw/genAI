# Prompt template for research briefing for an event

This prompt is designed to prepare for an event such as a panel discussion, conference, or speech.

## Template 1: more structured

```
# CONTEXT
I am a journalist covering the event below for [SPECIFY AUDIENCE AND OUTLET]. I have 1 hour to prepare.

# REQUIREMENTS
Produce a research briefing using the structure below. Give only the most essential, immediately useful information.
Be concise — bullet points and short sentences and paragraphs preferred. Skip anything that isn't directly relevant to covering this event today.
Include sources for each piece of information where I can verify it. Use quotation marks to indicate sentences that are quoted directly, and attribute.
If there is limited or no recent information on any question, or you are uncertain about the accuracy of any information, say so. Do not give answers to questions you are not confident about.

# THE EVENT
A panel discussion hosted by StudentMinds on University Mental Health Day (12 March), exploring how AI is affecting student learning, mental health, and human connection in higher education. Chaired by CEO Rosie Tressler OBE, with speakers: Reeya Shrestha (MA AI student), Tara J Murphy (Mental Health Mentor), Paul Bradshaw (MA Data Journalism, Birmingham City University), and Oli Harrison (AI & HE content specialist).

# THE BRIEFING
1. Quick-fire Org Briefing (5 minutes)
In 3–4 sentences each: Tell me about the organiser, and what is University Mental Health Day? Any notable recent activity or controversies (topical context) I should know about?
2. Speaker Snapshots (10 minutes)
One sentence per speaker — their most relevant credential or recent public position on the issue. 
3. The Two Key Facts (10 minutes)
Give two striking, citable statistics on the issue. Prioritise recent, reputable sources. 
4. The Core Tension (5 minutes)
In a short paragraph, summarise the central debate this event sits within — what do proponents and sceptics each argue?
5. Five Questions to Ask (10 minutes)
Sharp, specific questions I can put to speakers — ones that probe beyond the event's own narrative and look for news, accountability, or conflict.

```

## Template 2: more open

```
# CONTEXT
I am a journalist covering the event below for [SPECIFY AUDIENCE AND OUTLET]. I have 1 hour to prepare.

# REQUIREMENTS
Produce a research briefing using the structure below. Give only the most essential, immediately useful information.
Be concise — bullet points and short sentences and paragraphs preferred. Skip anything that isn't directly relevant to covering this event today.
Include sources for each piece of information where I can verify it. Use quotation marks to indicate sentences that are quoted directly, and attribute.
If there is limited or no recent information on any question, or you are uncertain about the accuracy of any information, say so. Do not give answers to questions you are not confident about.

# THE EVENT
A panel discussion hosted by StudentMinds on University Mental Health Day (12 March), exploring how AI is affecting student learning, mental health, and human connection in higher education. Chaired by CEO Rosie Tressler OBE, with speakers: Reeya Shrestha (MA AI student), Tara J Murphy (Mental Health Mentor), Paul Bradshaw (MA Data Journalism, Birmingham City University), and Oli Harrison (AI & HE content specialist).

# THE BRIEFING
Provide a breakdown of information the audience will need to make sense of the event:

* Who the key people are
* Who the organisers/hosts are
* Why is this important now? What is the topical context? (What has been happening recently to make this newsworthy)
* When were/are any key events related to this issue (e.g. recent changes, future plans)
* How does the system work?
* What are the key facts on the issues to be discussed?
* What jargon might be used that you need to understand and translate for your audience
* What is not known?
* What questions could you ask?
* Where the event is taking place (unless it’s virtual)
* Add anything else you think the audience will need to make sense of the event

```
